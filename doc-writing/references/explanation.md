Explanation is a **discursive treatment** of a subject that permits
_reflection_. Explanation is **understanding-oriented**.

Explanation deepens and broadens the reader's understanding. It brings clarity,
light, and context.

Reflection occurs _after_ something else and depends on something else, yet
brings something new — shines a new light — on the subject matter.

The perspective is higher and wider than the other three types. It doesn't take
the user's eye-level view (how-to) or a close-up view of the machinery
(reference). Its scope is a topic — "an area of knowledge" — bounded in a
reasonable, meaningful way.

For the user, explanation joins things together. It answers: _Can you tell me
about ...?_

Explanation is the only kind of documentation it might make sense to read away
from the product itself — in the bath, as it were.

## The Value and Place of Explanation

### Explanation and Understanding

Explanation is characterised by its distance from the practitioner's active
concerns. It doesn't have direct implications for what they do. This makes it
sometimes seem less important — but that's a mistake. It may be less _urgent_
than the other three, but it's no less _important_. It's not a luxury.

No practitioner can afford to be without understanding of their craft.
Understanding seals together the other components of mastery and makes it safely
one's own. Without it, knowledge is loose, fragmented, fragile, and practice is
_anxious_.

The word _explanation_ refers to _unfolding_ — revelation of what is hidden in
the folds. Words meaning _understanding_ share roots with _to hold or grasp_ (as
in _comprehend_).

Alternative names for explanation sections:

- _Discussion_
- _Background_
- _Conceptual guides_
- _Topics_

### Explanation and Its Boundaries

Explanation is often not explicitly recognised in documentation. Instead it
tends to be scattered in small parcels in other sections.

It's not always easy to write. Where does one start? There's an open-endedness
that gives the writer too many possibilities.

Tutorials, how-to guides, and reference are clearly scoped by something
well-defined: what the user needs to learn, the task to achieve, or the machine
itself. For explanation, it's useful to have a real or imagined _why_ question
as a prompt. Otherwise, draw reasonable lines and be satisfied.

## Writing Good Explanation

### Make Connections

Help weave a web of understanding. **Make connections** to other things, even
outside the immediate topic, if that helps.

### Provide Context

**Provide background and context**: explain _why_ things are so — design
decisions, historical reasons, technical constraints. Draw implications, mention
specific examples.

### Talk _About_ the Subject

Explanation guides are _about_ a topic in the sense that they are _around_ it.
Names should reflect this — you should be able to place an implicit _about_ in
front of each title:

- _About user authentication_
- _About database connection policies_

Things to discuss:

- The bigger picture
- History
- Choices, alternatives, possibilities
- Why: reasons and justifications

### Admit Opinion and Perspective

All human activity and knowledge is invested within opinion. The reality of any
creation is rich with opinion, and that needs to be part of understanding.

Any understanding comes from a perspective — other perspectives exist.
**Explanation can and must consider alternatives**, counter-examples, or
multiple approaches to the same question.

You're not giving instruction or describing facts — you're opening up the topic
for consideration. Think of explanation as discussion: discussions can even
weigh up contrary _opinions_.

### Keep Explanation Closely Bounded

One risk: explanation tends to absorb other things. The writer feels the urge to
include instruction or technical description. But documentation has other places
for these. Allowing them to creep in interferes with the explanation and removes
them from their correct place.

## Language Patterns

| Pattern                                                                | Purpose                                                                    |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| _The reason for x is because historically, y ..._                      | Explain                                                                    |
| _W is better than z, because ..._                                      | Offer judgements and opinions where appropriate                            |
| _An x in system y is analogous to a w in system z. However ..._        | Provide context                                                            |
| _Some users prefer w (because z). This can be a good approach, but..._ | Weigh up alternatives                                                      |
| _An x interacts with a y as follows: ..._                              | Unfold internal secrets to help understand why something does what it does |

## The Food Analogy: On Food and Cooking

Harold McGee's _On Food and Cooking_ (1984) doesn't teach how to cook. It
contains no recipes (except as historical examples) and isn't reference. It
places food and cooking in the context of history, society, science, and
technology. It explains _why_ we do what we do in the kitchen and how that has
changed.

It's not a book to read _while_ cooking. You read it when you want to reflect.
It illuminates by taking multiple perspectives, shining light from different
angles.

After reading it, understanding is changed. Knowledge is richer and deeper. What
you learned may or may not be immediately applicable, but _it will change how
you think about your craft, and will affect your practice_.
